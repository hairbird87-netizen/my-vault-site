Refining definitions: 

Liquidity is a term that derives and is made relevant because of the actual transactions that occurred on the market and what appears on the DOM, also known as the order book. Real liquidity refers to trades that actually took place, and fake liquidity refers to spoofs that appear on the DOM. Therefore liquidity refers to money being traded everywhere on the charts; however, by using terms such as "liquidity pools" or "areas of liquidity," it focuses on key levels where the quantity of traders increased, whether it is the actual trades that took place or the algorithms that are contributing to the fake liquidity through spoofing. In a more practical sense. Real liquidity is often what is referred to for most applications, as that is what candlesticks are generated from. Furthermore, FVG, swing highs, swing lows, and smart money do not consider fake liquidity, as they cannot see orders that could happen (the DOM) but rather predict them by going off of what is already there (the real liquidity). Moreover, this proves the DOMS' usefulness in detecting icebergs. Institutions breaking up large orders into smaller chunks in order to hide the true size of the position.
